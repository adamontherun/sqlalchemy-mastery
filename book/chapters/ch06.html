Chapter 6 · Part II — The ORM

Querying with the ORM

Here's the payoff of learning Core first: there is no "ORM query language" to learn. It's the same select(); you just put classes in it and get objects out.

select(Entity) and the scalars() habit

The one wrinkle worth understanding: session.execute(select(Film)) returns rows, tuples of one Film each, i.e. (Film,), because a select can name several things. Since you usually want just the objects, session.scalars() unwraps the first element of each row. Internalize the pair: execute() for rows, scalars() for objects, and 90% of "why is my result a tuple?" confusion never happens.

Model attributes are the same column expressions you used in Chapter 3: Film.year >= 2016, Film.title.ilike("%dune%"), Film.id.in_([1, 2, 3]) all compile exactly as before.

Aggregates and mixed selections

Set-based writes: ORM-enabled UPDATE and DELETE

Chapter 5's load-mutate-commit is right for business logic on individual objects. For "touch ten thousand rows" it would be a disaster, so the ORM also accepts Core's update()/delete() directly on the session:

The ORM-enabled versions do something Core alone can't: they also update or evict matching objects already loaded in the session, keeping your in-memory world consistent with what you just did to the database.

Upserts: when "insert or update" must be atomic

The check-then-insert alternative (SELECT, then decide) has a race window between the check and the write; two concurrent requests both pass the check, one crashes. ON CONFLICT moves the decision into the database where it's atomic. Note the import: upsert syntax is deliberately dialect-specific (this one lives in sqlalchemy.dialects.postgresql), because every database spells it differently, and SQLAlchemy refuses to paper over semantics that differ.

⚠ Gotcha

ORM-enabled bulk UPDATE/DELETE and upserts do not fire cascades or most ORM events. They're set-based SQL, and rows the statement touches were never loaded. If your model relies on Python-side delete-orphan cascade behavior (Chapter 7) or event listeners, those only run through the unit of work (load-and-mutate), not through bulk statements. Know which mode you're in.

When the ORM is the wrong tool

A quiet rule of thumb to close the chapter: the ORM earns its overhead when identity and behavior matter: the same row seen twice must be the same object, changes should be tracked, cascades should fire. For read-heavy reporting where rows go straight into JSON or a DataFrame, select plain columns (select(Track.artist, ...)) or drop to a Connection, where there's no identity map to fill and no objects to construct. Chapter 11 puts numbers on this.

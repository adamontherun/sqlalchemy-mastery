Chapter 7 · Part II — The ORM

Relationships

relationship() turns foreign keys into object navigation: book.author, author.books. It's the ORM's best feature and the source of its most famous performance bug. This chapter gives you both the modeling and the loader discipline.

One-to-many, both directions

Three things to notice. The ForeignKey column still exists; relationships are built on the FK, not instead of it. But you'll rarely assign author_id by hand again: set book.author or append to author.books and the flush works out the keys. back_populates names the attribute on the other class, and SQLAlchemy keeps the two in sync in Python immediately: set book.author = le_guin and the book is already in le_guin.books, before any SQL exists. And one session.add(author) saves the whole graph, since the save-update cascade follows relationships to find related pending objects.

cascade="all, delete-orphan" encodes an ownership claim: books belong to their author. Delete the author and the books go too (delete); remove a book from the collection and the orphaned row is deleted rather than left dangling with a NULL FK (delete-orphan). For relationships that are mere references, a book's publisher say, you'd omit the cascade; don't claim ownership you don't mean.

Many-to-many: the association table

secondary= points at the join table and SQLAlchemy manages its rows invisibly: append a tag, a row appears; remove it, the row goes. The moment the association needs data of its own (added_at, who added it), promote it to a real mapped class and the m2m becomes two one-to-manys, the "association object" pattern.

The N+1 problem, with a body count

Relationships load lazily by default: first access emits a SELECT. Innocent in a script; a massacre in a loop. examples/ch07/03_n_plus_one.py counts actual statements with an event listener:

Eleven queries for ten bands: one for the bands, plus one per band the moment the loop touches band.albums. It works, it's correct, and at 10,000 rows it's a production incident with your name on it. The fixes are loader options: same query, different loading plan:

LoaderSQL shapeUse for
selectinload(A.items) 2nd SELECT with IN (parent ids) Collections, the default answer
joinedload(A.parent) LEFT OUTER JOIN, same statement Many-to-one: one extra column set, no row explosion
raiseload(A.items) no SQL, raises instead Enforcement: turn hidden IO into loud errors
lazyload / default SELECT on first access Genuinely rare access on single objects
⚠ Gotcha

joinedload() on a collection multiplies parent rows by the join (10 bands × 3 albums = 30 result rows), so SQLAlchemy requires session.scalars(stmt).unique().all() to de-duplicate, and raises a very explicit error if you forget. That's the deeper reason the rule of thumb exists: selectinload for collections, joinedload for many-to-ones. Each is the shape that doesn't distort the result set.

🏭 In production

N+1s are invisible in development: 3 test rows load fast no matter what. Two habits catch them before customers do: run dev with echo=True occasionally and read the flood, and put raiseload("*") on query paths that must be IO-complete (or globally in tests; Chapter 10 shows the fixture). The async world goes further: over there, an un-eager-loaded relationship access typically raises MissingGreenlet instead of silently emitting SQL, because the implicit blocking IO a lazy load needs can't happen inside a coroutine. Chapter 8 explains why that accident of implementation is a feature in practice.

Ordering and sensible defaults

Collections come back in database order, i.e., no order at all. Declare one where it's intrinsic: relationship(..., order_by="Player.goals.desc()"). And for large collections consider whether you want a relationship at all: select(Album).where(Album.band_id == b.id).limit(...) beats loading ten thousand albums to show five. (2.0 also offers lazy="write_only" for exactly this: a relationship you can append to but never accidentally load.)
